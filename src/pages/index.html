Hiring neurodiversity people can benefit your company!

(Site name) is aimed to help companies to educate hiring teams on the best ways to be inclusive and conduct tech interviews to avoid missing out on qualified candidates who may learn and interact differently.

If you’re a savvy IT exec or hiring professional, and you must be if you’re here, then you’ve likely heard a little already about this neurodiversity, or Cognitive Diversity, thing. Maybe you’ve even read “Neurodiversity as a Competitive Advantage”(link) in the Harvard Business Review (HBR) and you’re wondering, “How can I tap into this in demand skills base?” If you’re not quite sure what I’m talking about, don’t worry, you’ll be hip to this emerging innovation by the time you leave here. More than that you’ll learn how to bring Cognitive Diversity to your technology teams, joining the ranks of innovators like Microsoft, Ford, IBM, Chase and more (Austin and Pisano, HBR) who are seeking out this minority population and experiencing great results for their efforts.

What is this Cognitive Diversity thing anyway? We’ll start with our nearly synonymous sister term neurodiversity. “Nurodiversity is the idea that neurological differences like Autism and ADHD are the result of normal, natural variation in the human genome” (Robinson, Psychology Today). Neurodiverse people, like those on the Autism spectrum, with diagnosis like dyspraxia, dyslexia, ADHD and more are differently abled than our neurotypical peers. Cognitive Diversity, for the purposes of this site, goes a step further to include those who may identify strongly with the term disability and have concerns that the neurodiversity movement could inadvertently erase the very real struggles they face that other groups may not. This can include people with any of the diagnoses above and loop in those with sometimes trauma induced differences like PTSD, depression, anxiety and others. Like all populations, there isn’t really agreement on the question of labels (we are all different after all), but imo that’s ok.

What’s important here is this: those who are neurologically different than the majority have skills to share, strengths and weaknesses like everyone else, but the world as we all know turns for the majority. The technology sector is no different and in fact it might even be one of the worst offenders. As a result this amazing population of workers are struggling to get into the jobs for which they are most qualified. Let me say this next piece really loud so we’re clear, “The standard tech interview process itself, designed by and for the neurotypical mind, is keeping Cognitive Diversity out of your company”. Our goal here at Hiring Cognitive is to help you remedy that problem.

Priority 1

Defining Cognitive Diversity (why you want to hire them)

What is cognitive diversity?

Cognitive diversity is an umbrella term for changes that occur within the brain that are considered to be normal variations. These changes allow people to process information in different and often new and creative ways. This means that cognitive diversity is largely a positive thing.

It is important to have a diverse team where employees know they can easily access tools or equitable accommodations or options effectively (and without judgment). This acceptance can increase creativity, motivation, and consequently productivity. Accepting differences allows for a more equitable world. People may process, learn, remember and convey information differently.

Priority 2

Suggestions / Tips

See the organizational flow created by Liz in Figma

Candidates should be asked how to they prefer to learn/process and choose from examples or a few different options. Hiring teams should think about hiring someone because of their skills and not to be compliant with the law. People should come first than their neurodiversity and the company in a nonjudgmental and equitable environment conduct the interview to find qualified candidates that may learn differently.

Everyone should have the same right to show their capabilities at work. An equity hiring process will make this possible!

(maybe) walk through interview by role, and “suggest a better process” with each breakdown. Pull details from interviews and resource articles.

Here we have some tips to help you to conduct a better interview:

  • Don’t have assignments to complete in a certain time
  • Don’t request the candidate to identify themselves, for example: are you proactive, etc
  • Don’t have excess stimulation in the room
  • More humane process and less automated
  • The hiring team should see the candidate’s qualities/skills before their diversity
  • Larger deadlines for neurodiversity candidates
  • make the neurodivergent candidate feel confident
  • The company needs to show the candidate that they contacted them because of the skills and not to comply with the law
  • companies should empower people to talk more about neurodiversity
  • Don’t have an interview with a lot of people at the same time
  • Don’t interview in a noisy environment and when people are constantly moving
  •  video interview instead of the phone interview for the first round
  • Non interview-based test
